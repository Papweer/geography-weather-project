Understanding Blizzards

Understanding Blizzards

The met office defines a blizzard as severe winter storm characterised by strong, sustained winds. (typically exceeding 48 km/h), significant snowfall or blowing snow, and reduced visibility (less than 400 metres) for a duration of at least three hours.

These conditions can lead to hazardous travel, extreme cold, and reduced visibility, making blizzards one of the most dangerous winter weather phenomena in the UK, though they occur less frequently than in other regions.

Unlike regular snowstorms, blizzards are defined primarily by their wind speed and visibility reduction rather than by the amount of snowfall. In fact, a blizzard can occur without any falling snow if strong winds pick up and blow around snow that has already fallen—a common occurrence in parts of northern England and Scotland.

Blizzard Criteria:

  • • Winds ≥ 48 km/h
  • • Visibility < 400 metres
  • • Duration ≥ 3 hours

Types of Severe Winter Weather

Ground Blizzard

Occurs when strong winds lift and blow loose snow that has already fallen. No new snowfall is needed, just existing snow and powerful winds. Common in exposed upland areas of Scotland and Northern England.

Freezing Rain

Rain that falls through a shallow layer of cold temperatures near the surface, allowing the raindrops to freeze on contact with surfaces creating a coating of ice. A significant hazard on UK roads and railways.

Atlantic Storms

Low-pressure systems from the Atlantic can bring heavy snowfall and strong winds to the UK, especially affecting western regions and higher ground in Wales, Scotland, and northern England.

Beast from the East

A weather pattern bringing cold air from Siberia and Russia to the UK. When this cold air meets Atlantic weather systems, it can cause significant snowfall across the country, as seen in 2018.

Historical Context in the UK

While true blizzards are relatively rare in the UK compared to North America or parts of continental Europe, we have experienced several significant winter weather events that have caused major disruptions.

Notable UK Winter Weather Events
  • • Winter of 1962-63 - Known as the "Big Freeze," one of the coldest winters on record with snow lying for months in some areas.
  • • January 1978 - Severe blizzards across the south and southwest of England trapped thousands of motorists overnight.
  • • January 1987 - Southeast England experienced its heaviest snowfall since 1962-63, with up to 30cm in places.
  • • February 2018 ("Beast from the East") - Brought widespread snow and sub-zero temperatures across the UK, combining with Storm Emma to create blizzard conditions in many areas.

Effects of Severe Winter Weather in the UK

While true blizzards are less common in the UK than in other parts of the world, severe winter weather can still have widespread impacts on communities, infrastructure, and daily life across Britain.

Health Impacts

  • • Hypothermia and frostbite in extreme conditions
  • • Increased risk of slips and falls on icy surfaces
  • • Heart attacks from overexertion (e.g., snow clearing)
  • • Increased pressure on NHS and emergency services
  • • Excess winter deaths, particularly among the elderly

Infrastructure

  • • Power outages affecting thousands of homes
  • • Water supply issues from frozen or burst pipes
  • • Telecommunications disruptions
  • • School closures across affected regions
  • • Strain on local council resources for gritting and snow clearing

Transportation

  • • Stranded motorists on motorways and rural roads
  • • Rail service cancellations and delays
  • • Airport closures and flight cancellations
  • • Public transport disruption in towns and cities
  • • Isolated rural communities cut off from services

Economic

  • • Business closures and lost productivity
  • • Supply chain disruption affecting supermarkets
  • • Agricultural losses, particularly livestock in upland areas
  • • Increased energy demand and rising heating costs
  • • Significant costs to local authorities for winter maintenance

Regional Impacts Across the UK

Scotland and Northern England

Areas more accustomed to severe winter weather but still vulnerable:

  • • Frequent isolation of rural Highland communities
  • • Major trunk roads like the M8, A9, and A1 often severely affected
  • • Higher risk to livestock farming in upland areas
Southern England and Urban Areas

Less frequent severe winter weather but often greater disruption when it occurs:

  • • Transport networks quickly overwhelmed by relatively small amounts of snow
  • • Higher population density creating greater strain on services
  • • Significant economic impact from business and commuter disruption

Long-Term Considerations

Severe winter weather in the UK raises several longer-term issues:

  • • Winter resilience - Questions about UK infrastructure preparation for infrequent but severe events.
  • • Local authority funding - Budget constraints affecting winter maintenance capabilities.
  • • Fuel poverty - Highlighting issues of affordable heating during prolonged cold periods.
  • • Climate change adaptation - Preparing for potentially more extreme and unpredictable winter weather patterns.

UK Winter Weather Safety Guide

Severe winter weather in the UK, while less frequent than in some countries, requires proper preparation and safety measures. This guide provides UK-specific advice for staying safe during snow, ice and blizzard conditions.

Before Severe Weather Arrives

Home Preparation
  • • Insulate your home - Check loft insulation, draught-proof windows and doors, and lag exposed water pipes.
  • • Stock essential supplies - Keep a few days' worth of food, water, and medications.
  • • Check heating system - Service your boiler annually and know how to operate your heating controls efficiently.
  • • Battery-powered radio - Essential for updates if power fails and mobile networks are disrupted.
Vehicle Preparation
  • • Winter check - Ensure antifreeze, battery, tyres, wipers and lights are ready for winter conditions.
  • • Keep fuel tank topped up - Don't let it fall below half in winter to prevent condensation freezing in fuel lines.
  • • Emergency kit - Include ice scraper, de-icer, torch, blankets, high-visibility vest, and shovel.
  • • Check tyres - Ensure at least 3mm tread depth for winter driving (legal minimum is 1.6mm).

During Severe Winter Weather

At Home
  • • Heat your home efficiently - Keep main living room around 18-21°C and other rooms at least 16°C.
  • • Check on vulnerable neighbours - Especially the elderly or those living alone.
  • • If pipes freeze - Turn off water at the stopcock and thaw pipes gradually with warm (not hot) water or towels.
  • • Carbon monoxide awareness - Ensure proper ventilation with any fuel-burning heaters and have working CO detectors.
  • • Monitor Met Office updates - Check for updated weather warnings and forecasts.
When Travelling
  • • Consider if journey is essential - Postpone travel if Met Office has issued warnings.
  • • Plan your route - Stick to major roads more likely to be cleared and gritted.
  • • If stranded - Stay with your vehicle, call for help, run engine occasionally for heat (ensure exhaust is clear).
  • • Drive appropriately - Use higher gears, keep speeds down, leave 10 times normal stopping distance on snow/ice.
  • • Check public transport - Verify service status before travelling by rail or bus.

After Severe Weather

  • • Clear snow safely - Push rather than lift, take regular breaks, wear appropriate footwear.
  • • Check your property - Look for damage to roof, potential water ingress from melting snow.
  • • Clear paths and pavements - Clear snow, apply grit/salt (not hot water, which can refreeze).
  • • Watch for signs of hypothermia - Confusion, slurred speech, drowsiness require immediate medical attention.
  • • Prepare for potential flooding - As temperatures rise, rapid snowmelt can cause flooding issues.
